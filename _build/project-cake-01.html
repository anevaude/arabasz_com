Overview

As an early-stage startup with a tiny team, the app had quickly evolved from a scrappy prototype to a tool that was being used by hundreds of users. We needed to move quickly to make the app look more polished and trustworthy.

As the company's first Frontend Developer, I had the exciting opportunity to review our app and suggest improvements for usability, mobile-friendliness, and overall appearance.

Team & Role

I served as both a Front End Developer and Designer, working closely with the team to make decisions and deploy them straight-to-code.

In addition to myself, the team included two Back End Developers and a Product Owner.

Global improvements

Trust and safety were a top need for Cake users, who often uploaded sensitive documents and shared highly personal details about their end-of-life wishes. I felt it was important for the site to feel authentic and trustworthy. My first goal was to make the site look polished, cohesive and reflective of the company brand. Some highlights:

  • Brand Integration: Added the company logo to the navigation and used the brand's colors (light teal, dark teal, and cream) to make the website look more official and less like a prototype.
  • Icon Tweaks: Updated box-like icons that resembled checkboxes, which could confuse users. Also, removed ambiguous category icons from the navigation.
  • Simplified IA: As a developer, it was clear to me that the nav had grown over the years to accommodate a rapidly changing number of tools. I proposed a new sitemap that addressed links that were redundant or ambiguous, merging the bulk of the "Discover" child links into a single parent named "Checklist".





Improvements to the Discover page

Every new user started off with the "Discover" tool, so it was important that we get this part right. The tool was designed as a virtual deck of cards that a user can flip through and answer crucial questions about their healthcare and funeral/burial preferences. Key changes included:

  • Card-like Aesthetics: Redesigned the "Discover" tool to emulate a deck of cards, incorporating rounded corners and themed borders to enhance the visual resemblance to a playing card.
  • Swipe Functionality: Expanded user interaction by introducing swipe gestures. In addition to the existing "Back" and "Save" buttons, touch-activated arrows were integrated, allowing seamless swiping on mobile and tablet devices.
  • Touch-friendly fields: Improved mobile usability by enlarging answer buttons, meeting minimum recommended sizing for touch points. Iconography was also introduced to clearly indicate yes/no answers.





Improvements to the Documents page

The Documents page served as the platform for users to upload vital files such as Wills or Health Care Proxy forms. A notable concern was the rigid design that assigned specific slots for document uploads (e.g., Wills could only be uploaded to the "Wills" box). This assumed users (a) had all documents ready, (b) knew each document's purpose, and (c) had no need for uploading documents beyond the provided slots. To address these limitations, I proposed the following changes:

  • Flexible Document Upload: Instead of confining users to pre-assigned slots, I recommended a more versatile approach. Users could now upload any document they deemed important and subsequently categorize it (i.e. Type) according to their preferences. This proved especially beneficial for cases where users needed to upload files that didn't match our predefined slots.
  • Revamped layout A shift from the original design involved adopting a file list pattern akin to Dropbox or Google Drive, a format consistently recognized and used for years. This not only presented a cleaner and more familiar display of files but also proved to be more intuitive. Additionally, this modification significantly reduced the vertical space required to showcase files, preventing a potentially frustrating user experience of endless scrolling.

Mobile Optimization

The app was originally designed with desktop users in mind, but it was 2018 and I had a feeling we were leaving out a significant mobile audience.

I used Google Analytics to confirm that 64-68% of users who visited the Cake homepage from a mobile or tablet device. This data underscored the pressing need to prioritize mobile-friendliness to better serve the app's audience and stay competitive.
